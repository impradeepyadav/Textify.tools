Beyond the Buttons: Real Title Case Rules That Matter

Most free converters apply a simplistic “capitalize every word” rule. That might work for email subject lines, but it's wrong for academic writing, journalism, and publishing. Here's a quick look at the four major style guides and what they actually expect:

  • APA Style (American Psychological Association) – Capitalize the first word of the title and subtitle, all nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and subordinating conjunctions (like “because”, “although”). Lowercase articles (a, an, the), coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or), and all prepositions – yes, even four‑letter ones like “with” or “from”. The only exception is the first or last word of the title.
  • Chicago Style (Chicago Manual of Style) – Very similar to APA, but Chicago goes a step further and lowercases *all* prepositions, regardless of length. Words like “between” or “through” stay lowercase unless they're the first or last word.
  • MLA Style (Modern Language Association) – Capitalize the first and last words and all principal words. Articles, coordinating conjunctions, and prepositions are lowercased. MLA doesn't care about preposition length; “under”, “above”, “along” – all lowercase.
  • AP Style (Associated Press) – A different beast. Capitalize all words with four or more letters, plus the first and last words. Short conjunctions and prepositions (three letters or fewer) are lowercase. This gives a very clean, news‑ready look that works beautifully for press releases and blog posts.

Don't worry about memorizing all this. Click a button, and the tool applies the exact rules for you.

Common Title Case Mistakes (We Made Them So You Don't Have To)

  1. Capitalizing short prepositions in APA. "With" should stay lowercase in APA unless it's the first word. Many writers capitalize it anyway because it feels important. Trust the style guide, not your gut.
  2. Forgetting that the last word is always capitalized. Even a tiny word like "up" gets a capital letter if it's the last word. Our tool never misses this.
  3. Using sentence case for headlines. Sentence case (only the first word capitalized) has its place in emails, but for article titles and blog posts, title case increases readability and click‑through rates.
  4. Ignoring brand names. You never want "iPhone" to become "Iphone". That's why we added the Custom Terms feature – add your brand words once, and they'll stay protected across all conversions.
