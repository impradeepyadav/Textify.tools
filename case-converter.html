Understanding the 14 Case Styles

Choosing the right case can make a significant difference in how professional and readable your content appears. Here is a brief explanation of each style available in our tool:

  • UPPERCASE – Every single letter becomes a capital. Useful for acronyms, headings, or when you need to emphasise something strongly (though long passages in all caps can be hard to read).
  • lowercase – All letters are small. Ideal for email addresses, URLs, or normalising data for analysis.
  • Title Case – The first letter of each major word is capitalised. Follows APA and Chicago style conventions for headlines, article titles, and book names.
  • Sentence case – Only the first word of the text starts with a capital, just like a normal sentence. Perfect for paragraphs and body copy.
  • Capitalized – Only the very first character becomes uppercase, and everything else remains exactly as it was. Great for names where you just want to ensure the first letter is correct.
  • tOGGLE cASE – Every letter flips its current case – lowercase letters become uppercase and vice versa. Often used for memes or attention‑grabbing social media posts.
  • InVeRt CaSe – Similar to toggle case, but often applied as a stylistic choice on platforms like Twitter or Instagram.
  • RaNdOm CaSe – Each character is randomly turned into uppercase or lowercase. It adds a playful, unpredictable feel to your text.
  • AlTeRnAtInG – The case alternates with every single character, creating a distinctive pattern that stands out in feeds and comments.
  • camelCase – Spaces are removed, and the first letter of each word (except the very first) is capitalised. This format is widely used in programming, for example: userLoginCount.
  • PascalCase – Like camelCase, but the first word is also capitalised. Commonly seen in class names such as CustomerRecord.
  • snake_case – All letters are lowercase, and spaces are replaced with underscores. Popular in Python code and database column names: user_login_count.
  • kebab-case – Similar to snake_case but uses hyphens instead of underscores. Frequently found in URLs and CSS class names: user-login-count.
  • CONSTANT_CASE – Everything is uppercase with underscores between words. Used for defining constants in programming: MAX_LOGIN_ATTEMPTS.
